# Why I'm building this

Since 2012, when I developed my first computational epidemiology simulation
analyzing a theoretical Chilean earthquake with a budding bioinformatician,
I've been building geospatial and geostatistical software: mapping tools,
spatial databases, geocoding pipelines, mostly for people who need a map to
answer a question, not to make one. Along the way, I kept running into the
same problem from different angles: small health departments and rural
jurisdictions doing real epidemiology without the tooling that comes
standard at a state health department or university. A commercial GIS
license is expensive. Tools such as SaTScan are free but have their own
learning curves. Cloud geocoding APIs mean exporting sensitive addresses to
a third party, which is a nonstarter for a lot of health data.

I'm an MPH candidate in epidemiology now, finishing up a practicum with a
rural health department, and MESA (Mapping Epidemiology for Sparse Areas)
came out of watching that gap up close: a jurisdiction with real disease
clusters worth finding and no easy way to find them without either paying
for tools built with a much bigger budget in mind, or building something
themselves.

So that's where MESA fits in. Offline geocoding and spatial cluster
detection, built to run on one laptop with one dataset, validated the way
I'd want a tool validated if I were the one trusting its output for a real
investigation. I enjoy building things, and I've been the analyst staring
at a shapefile at 11pm wishing the tool in front of me was simpler, and
more honest about what it does and does not know. That's the reason, more
than any belief that everyone else is doing it wrong.

I am also developing this application in part to assist me with completing
my Master's thesis, entitled "Spatiotemporal Modeling of Mosquito
Surveillance Activity and West Nile Virus Positivity Using Environmental
Covariates in California." I intend to implement features such as various
raster analyses, ordinary and Bayesian kriging, Bayesian modeling, and more.

I do hope this tool proves useful for the scientific community, as much as
it already has for me.
